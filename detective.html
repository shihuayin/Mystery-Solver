DETECTIVE NOVELIST & WORKS

Arthur Conan Doyle

Sir Arthur Conan Doyle (1859-1930) was a renowned British writer best known for creating the fictional detective Sherlock Holmes. Born on May 22, 1859, in Edinburgh, Scotland, Doyle was educated as a physician and practiced medicine for a time. However, his true passion lay in writing, and he eventually abandoned his medical career to pursue literature full-time.

Doyle's most famous literary creation, Sherlock Holmes, made his first appearance in the novel "A Study in Scarlet" (1887). Holmes quickly became an iconic character in detective fiction, known for his exceptional deductive reasoning and keen observation skills. The character's popularity soared with subsequent works, including "The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes" (1892) and "The Hound of the Baskervilles" (1902).

Agatha Christie

Agatha Christie (1890-1976) was a British author widely regarded as the "Queen of Mystery" and one of the most significant writers in the detective fiction genre. Born on September 15, 1890, in Torquay, Devon, England, Christie's works have had an unparalleled impact on crime fiction and continue to captivate readers around the world.

Over the course of her career, Agatha Christie penned numerous iconic works, including "Murder on the Orient Express" (1934), "Death on the Nile" (1937), "And Then There Were None" (1939), and "The Murder of Roger Ackroyd" (1926). Her mystery novels were known for their ingenious plots, unexpected twists, and unforgettable characters, making her a best-selling author during her lifetime and beyond.

Raymond Chandler

Raymond Chandler (1888-1959) was an influential American author, best known for his contributions to the hardboiled detective fiction genre. Born on July 23, 1888, in Chicago, Illinois, Chandler's works had a profound impact on the development of crime fiction and transformed the detective novel into a sophisticated and gritty art form.

Some of Chandler's other notable works include "Farewell, My Lovely" (1940), "The Long Goodbye" (1953), and "The Lady in the Lake" (1943). Through his writing, Chandler tackled themes of corruption, greed, and moral ambiguity, reflecting the societal anxieties of his time.

Edogawa Ranpo

Edogawa Ranpo (1894-1965) was a prominent Japanese author and a pioneer of modern mystery and detective fiction in Japan. Ranpo is often referred to as the "Father of Japanese Mystery Fiction." He played a crucial role in popularizing the mystery genre in Japan during the early 20th century, introducing elements of psychological suspense, macabre themes, and intricate puzzles in his stories. His distinctive writing style and unique storytelling techniques captivated readers, making him a literary icon.

One of Ranpo's most famous literary creations is the detective Kogoro Akechi, a brilliant and enigmatic sleuth who first appeared in the story "The Case of the Murder on D. Hill" (1925). Akechi became a recurring character in many of Ranpo's works and is often considered the Japanese equivalent of Sherlock Holmes.

Lawrence Block

Lawrence Block (born June 24, 1938) is a prolific and highly acclaimed American author, best known for his contributions to the crime fiction and mystery genres. Born in Buffalo, New York, Block's writing career spans over several decades, and he has earned a devoted following for his captivating storytelling and memorable characters.

Block's most famous and enduring creation is Matthew Scudder, an unlicensed private investigator and recovering alcoholic based in New York City. The character of Matthew Scudder first appeared in "The Sins of the Fathers" (1976) and went on to become the protagonist of numerous successful novels, including "A Walk Among the Tombstones" (1992) and "A Drop of the Hard Stuff" (2011).
